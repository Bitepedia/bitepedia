# Bitepedia

A bite-sized encyclopedia of food, visualized as vintage botanical specimen plates.

## What it is

Bitepedia turns any dish into a hand-drawn encyclopedia plate — the dish in the center, its components laid out around it like 19th-century botanical specimens. Click any element on the plate and Bitepedia drills into it: a noodle becomes a wheat flour ingredient page, a broth becomes a simmering process page, a sushi becomes a regional variation grid.

The point isn't a recipe app. It's a way of *seeing* food — atomized, annotated, and connected — that rewards curiosity instead of demanding it.

## Why it exists

Most food content treats a dish as a finished object: a photo, a recipe, a review. Bitepedia treats every dish as a graph — components, ingredients, processes, regional variations, historical evolution — and lets the viewer choose their own depth.

The closest reference point is the visual encyclopedia tradition (Diderot, Redouté, mid-century scientific illustration). The closest contemporary inspiration is the click-anywhere drilldown of generative interfaces. Bitepedia stitches the two.

## Core differentiation

Bitepedia is **subject-driven**, not layer-driven.

When you click a component on a Bitepedia plate, Bitepedia identifies *what that component is* (a piece of dough, a sauce, a cooking step) and renders the next plate accordingly. The viewer never picks "show me the anatomy / show me the process" — Bitepedia decides the right format for what was clicked, then shows it.
